About Me

Drawn to the World,
One Line at a Time

I am a designer who believes that observation precedes creation. My practice begins not at the drawing table, but in the streets, markets, and quiet corners of everyday life — studying how light falls, how shadows reveal structure, and how geometry underlies the organic.

Sketching is how I think. Each line is a decision; each composition, a question. Through visual storytelling, I translate moments of quiet observation into works that carry both intellectual rigour and emotional honesty.

I am drawn to the space between seeing and making — that moment of interpretation where a raw visual experience becomes a considered design response.

Inspiration

A late afternoon walk through the old city quarter. The interplay of shadows between close-set buildings created a natural one-point perspective that demanded documentation. The compression of space was the primary subject.

Challenges

Translating three-dimensional atmospheric haze into the flatness of ink required careful decisions about what to omit. The temptation to over-detail had to be consciously resisted — the sketch needed silence as much as marks.

Key Learning

Negative space is a design decision, not an absence. This piece taught me that the unmade mark is as expressive as the made one — a lesson that fundamentally altered how I approach composition.

Inspiration

A monsoon morning spent in a plant nursery, cataloguing how leaves arrange themselves for maximum light. The elegance of natural design — no element wasted, every form purposeful — became an extended metaphor for design itself.

Challenges

The temptation to over-annotate disrupted visual flow. Finding the balance between scientific precision and aesthetic openness required several unsuccessful drafts — a necessary exercise in editorial restraint.

Key Learning

Annotation is a form of design thinking. The decision about what to name, measure, and mark is not merely descriptive — it is an argument about what matters. Selecting what to annotate defines the intellectual position of the work.

Visual Observations

Studies in Form,
Light & Texture

These are not photographs — they are visual notes. Each image is a question asked of the world; a pattern observed, a rhythm felt, a structure revealed.

Rhythm in Repetition

The repeating geometry of balconies revealed patterns that later influenced perspective studies in the Urban Form series.

Light Through Water

Caustic patterns formed by light refracting through glass. A study in how indirect illumination creates movement and depth on flat surfaces.

Texture as Language

Tree bark observed as a record of time — growth, stress, and adaptation written in vertical and horizontal marks. Surface as autobiography.

Shadows as Form

The cast shadow of a simple building block revealed how light constructs secondary compositions on the ground — geometry answering geometry.

The Logic of Growth

Leaf venation as information architecture. The branching pattern distributes resources efficiently — a structural logic that mirrors network design principles.

Ascending Order

A staircase as typographic rhythm — regular intervals ascending, each step a beat, the whole a composition of controlled repetition and implied motion.

From Observation to Interpretation

Seeing,
then Making

The passage from photograph to sketch is not reproduction — it is translation. What is kept, what is discarded, and what is invented defines the design voice.

Photograph
Sketch

The Translation

The photograph captured the visual fact — buildings compressed in a telephoto frame, windows catalogued, surfaces described. The sketch asked a different question: what is the structural logic of this scene? By stripping away colour, material, and photographic tone, the sketch isolates the pure geometric dialogue between volumes. The vanishing point, invisible in the photograph, becomes the compositional anchor of the drawing. This is the difference between recording and understanding.

Photograph
Sketch

The Translation

The photograph was a record of presence — leaves as visual mass, colour, and texture. The sketch became a theory. By reducing the plant to line and annotation, the drawing asked: how does this organism organise itself? The phyllotaxy notation — the pattern of leaf arrangement — emerged as the true subject of the piece. The sketch didn't reproduce the plant; it made an argument about it. This shift from recording to theorising is, for me, the beginning of design thinking.
